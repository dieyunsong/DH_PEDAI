# 100 Science Fiction Novels — A Diverse Reading List

A curated list of 100 works of science fiction chosen to span the full breadth of the genre: across eras (from 1666 to 2024), across the globe (over 25 countries of origin), and across the people who have written it — women, LGBTQ+ authors, and writers from African, Asian, Latin American, Caribbean, Middle Eastern, and Indigenous traditions whose work has too often sat outside the standard canon.

A few honest notes on selection: "diversity" here is deliberately multi-dimensional — author background, geography, language of origin, subgenre, and era. A handful of entries are landmark novellas, collections, or anthologies rather than strict novels; these are flagged and included because the diverse history of SF runs through them. Authors generally appear once, even when their work could fit several sections.

---

## I. Pioneers & Foundational Voices

1. **The Blazing World** — Margaret Cavendish (1666). One of the earliest proto-SF works in English, by a Duchess writing utopian interplanetary travel a century before the genre had a name.
2. **Frankenstein** — Mary Shelley (1818). The novel often credited as the birth of science fiction, written by a teenager.
3. **Sultana's Dream** — Rokeya Sakhawat Hossain (1905). A Bengali Muslim feminist's gender-reversed utopia; a foundational work of non-Western and feminist SF *(novella)*.
4. **Herland** — Charlotte Perkins Gilman (1915). An all-women society interrogating gender and social design.
5. **We** — Yevgeny Zamyatin (1924, Russia). The dystopian template that influenced Orwell and Huxley.
6. **War with the Newts** — Karel Čapek (1936, Czechoslovakia). Biting satirical SF from the writer who coined "robot."
7. **Star Maker** — Olaf Stapledon (1937, UK). A cosmic-scale philosophical sweep across the life of the universe.

## II. Women Who Reshaped the Genre

8. **The Left Hand of Darkness** — Ursula K. Le Guin (1969). A landmark exploration of gender and ambisexuality on a frozen world.
9. **The Dispossessed** — Ursula K. Le Guin (1974). An "ambiguous utopia" weighing anarchism against capitalism.
10. **The Female Man** — Joanna Russ (1975). A formally radical feminist classic.
11. **Woman on the Edge of Time** — Marge Piercy (1976). A utopian/dystopian split-future grounded in class and psychiatry.
12. **Where Late the Sweet Birds Sang** — Kate Wilhelm (1976). Cloning and survival after ecological collapse.
13. **Dreamsnake** — Vonda N. McIntyre (1978). A healer's journey across a post-nuclear world.
14. **Up the Walls of the World** — James Tiptree Jr. (Alice Sheldon) (1978). First-contact SF from a writer who upended assumptions about who wrote it.
15. **Downbelow Station** — C.J. Cherryh (1981). Dense, political space-station opera.
16. **The Handmaid's Tale** — Margaret Atwood (1985, Canada). Theocratic dystopia and reproductive control.
17. **Grass** — Sheri S. Tepper (1989). Ecological and theological SF on an alien world.
18. **Doomsday Book** — Connie Willis (1992). Time-travel historians and twin pandemics.
19. **A Wrinkle in Time** — Madeleine L'Engle (1962). The genre-defining children's/YA SF adventure.
20. **The Memoirs of a Survivor** — Doris Lessing (1974, UK/Zimbabwe). Speculative collapse from a future Nobel laureate.

## III. Afrofuturism & the Black Diaspora

21. **Babel-17** — Samuel R. Delany (1966). Language as weapon and consciousness; a queer Black SF pioneer's breakout.
22. **Kindred** — Octavia E. Butler (1979). Time-travel reckoning with American slavery; a cornerstone of the genre.
23. **Parable of the Sower** — Octavia E. Butler (1993). Eerily prescient climate-and-collapse SF.
24. **Brown Girl in the Ring / Midnight Robber** — Nalo Hopkinson (2000, Jamaica/Canada). Caribbean folklore fused with interstellar SF *(Midnight Robber)*.
25. **Who Fears Death** — Nnedi Okorafor (2010, Nigeria/US). Post-apocalyptic Sudan, magic, and survival.
26. **Lagoon** — Nnedi Okorafor (2014). First contact off the coast of Lagos.
27. **The Fifth Season** — N.K. Jemisin (2015). Hugo-sweeping geological apocalypse and oppression.
28. **Rosewater** — Tade Thompson (2016, Nigeria/UK). Alien biotech in near-future Nigeria.
29. **An Unkindness of Ghosts** — Rivers Solomon (2017). A generation ship organized like the antebellum South.
30. **The Old Drift** — Namwali Serpell (2019, Zambia). A multigenerational Zambian epic blending history and SF.
31. **Riot Baby** — Tochi Onyebuchi (2020). Rage, prophecy, and American racism *(novella)*.
32. **A Master of Djinn** — P. Djèlí Clark (2021). Steampunk-fantasy SF in an alternate magical Cairo.

## IV. East Asian Science Fiction

33. **Inter Ice Age 4** — Kōbō Abe (1959, Japan). Often called Japan's first full SF novel.
34. **Japan Sinks** — Sakyō Komatsu (1973, Japan). Landmark national-disaster SF.
35. **Paprika** — Yasutaka Tsutsui (1993, Japan). Dream-invading psychotherapy tech.
36. **The Memory Police** — Yōko Ogawa (1994, Japan). Quiet, devastating SF about enforced forgetting.
37. **All You Need Is Kill** — Hiroshi Sakurazaka (2004, Japan). The time-loop war novel behind *Edge of Tomorrow*.
38. **The Three-Body Problem** — Liu Cixin (2008, China). The hard-SF first-contact epic that broke Chinese SF into the West.
39. **Harmony** — Project Itoh (2008, Japan). A health-obsessed utopia turned sinister.
40. **Waste Tide** — Chen Qiufan (2013, China). E-waste, labor, and biotech on a Chinese island.
41. **Vagabonds** — Hao Jingfang (2016, China). A philosophical Mars/Earth political SF by a Hugo-winning economist.
42. **Terminal Boredom** — Izumi Suzuki (2021, Japan). Pioneering feminist/counterculture SF, finally translated *(collection)*.
43. **Tower** — Bae Myung-hoon (2009, South Korea). Satirical linked SF set in a single skyscraper-nation.
44. **I'm Waiting for You** — Kim Bo-Young (2021, South Korea). Relativity, love, and time *(collection)*.
45. **Counterweight** — Djuna (2023, South Korea). Corporate intrigue around a space elevator.

## V. South & Southeast Asian Science Fiction

46. **Escape** — Manjula Padmanabhan (2008, India). A dystopia of gendered erasure.
47. **Leila** — Prayaag Akbar (2017, India). Caste, purity, and walls in a fractured near-future.
48. **Ambiguity Machines and Other Stories** — Vandana Singh (2018, India). Physicist-author's lyrical, philosophical SF *(collection)*.
49. **The Gurkha and the Lord of Tuesday** — Saad Z. Hossain (2019, Bangladesh). Djinn, AI, and climate collapse *(novella)*.
50. **The City Inside** — Samit Basu (2020, India). Influencers and surveillance in a near-future Delhi.
51. **Machinehood** — S.B. Divya (2021, India/US). Gig-economy labor and AI rights.
52. **The Saint of Bright Doors** — Vajra Chandrasekera (2023, Sri Lanka). Genre-bending, award-winning speculative fiction.
53. **Bangkok Wakes to Rain** — Pitchaya Sudbanthad (2019, Thailand). A drowning city across past and future.

## VI. Latin American & Caribbean Science Fiction

54. **The Invention of Morel** — Adolfo Bioy Casares (1940, Argentina). A virtual-reality puzzle decades ahead of its time.
55. **Ficciones** — Jorge Luis Borges (1944, Argentina). Foundational speculative labyrinths *(collection)*.
56. **Kalpa Imperial** — Angélica Gorodischer (1983, Argentina). A mythic imagined empire, translated by Le Guin.
57. **A Planet for Rent** — Yoss (2001, Cuba). Allegorical SF about a Cuba/Earth ruled by alien tourists.
58. **Turing's Delirium** — Edmundo Paz Soldán (2003, Bolivia). Cryptography and cyber-politics.
59. **The Best of All Possible Worlds** — Karen Lord (2013, Barbados). Post-cataclysm anthropological SF.
60. **Fever Dream** — Samanta Schweblin (2014, Argentina). Ecological dread in hallucinatory miniature.
61. **Tentacle** — Rita Indiana (2015, Dominican Republic). Queer, Afro-Caribbean time-travel and climate ruin.
62. **Tender Is the Flesh** — Agustina Bazterrica (2017, Argentina). Brutal dystopia of industrialized cannibalism.

## VII. Middle Eastern, North African & Arab Science Fiction

63. **Utopia** — Ahmed Khaled Towfik (2008, Egypt). A class-war thriller set in a walled future Egypt.
64. **Frankenstein in Baghdad** — Ahmed Saadawi (2013, Iraq). A creature stitched from bombing victims; Booker International–shortlisted.
65. **The Queue** — Basma Abdel Aziz (2013, Egypt). Kafkaesque post-uprising bureaucratic dystopia.
66. **Otared** — Mohammad Rabie (2014, Egypt). A violent, occupied near-future Cairo.
67. **Iraq + 100** — ed. Hassan Blasim (2016, Iraq). Iraqi writers imagine 2103 *(anthology)*.
68. **Palestine + 100** — ed. Basma Ghalayini (2019, Palestine). Palestinian futures a century on *(anthology)*.

## VIII. Indigenous Futurisms

69. **Walking the Clouds** — ed. Grace L. Dillon (2012). The anthology that named "Indigenous futurisms" *(anthology)*.
70. **The Marrow Thieves** — Cherie Dimaline (2017, Métis, Canada). Dream-harvesting dystopia and Indigenous survival.
71. **Future Home of the Living God** — Louise Erdrich (2017, Ojibwe, US). Reverse-evolution and reproductive control.
72. **Moon of the Crusted Snow** — Waubgeshig Rice (2018, Anishinaabe, Canada). A northern community after the grid goes dark.
73. **Trail of Lightning** — Rebecca Roanhorse (2018, US). Post-flood monster-hunting on a reborn Diné homeland.
74. **A Snake Falls to Earth** — Darcie Little Badger (2021, Lipan Apache, US). Two worlds, Lipan storytelling, and ecological stakes.

## IX. LGBTQ+ & Queer Science Fiction

75. **The Long Way to a Small, Angry Planet** — Becky Chambers (2014). Warm, queer-normative found-family space opera.
76. **All the Birds in the Sky** — Charlie Jane Anders (2016). Magic vs. tech, by a leading trans SF voice.
77. **Ninefox Gambit** — Yoon Ha Lee (2016). Math-as-warfare military SF from a queer, trans Korean-American author.
78. **Autonomous** — Annalee Newitz (2017). Pharma piracy and the gender of robots.
79. **The Tea Master and the Detective** — Aliette de Bodard (2018, France/Vietnam). A sentient ship and a detective in a Vietnamese-coded galaxy *(novella)*.
80. **Gideon the Ninth** — Tamsyn Muir (2019, New Zealand). Lesbian necromancers in space.
81. **A Memory Called Empire** — Arkady Martine (2019). Identity, empire, and longing; a Hugo-winning space opera.
82. **The Seep** — Chana Porter (2020). A gentle alien takeover seen through a trans woman's grief.
83. **The Echo Wife** — Sarah Gailey (2021). Cloning, ambition, and marriage gone wrong.
84. **Light from Uncommon Stars** — Ryka Aoki (2021). Trans-affirming SF mixing violins, demons, and a refugee starship.

## X. European & Russian Science Fiction in Translation

85. **Solaris** — Stanisław Lem (1961, Poland). The definitive "unknowable alien" novel.
86. **Roadside Picnic** — Arkady & Boris Strugatsky (1972, USSR). The Zone, the Stalkers, and incomprehensible alien debris.
87. **The Slynx** — Tatyana Tolstaya (2000, Russia). Post-apocalyptic satire of culture and memory.
88. **The Possibility of an Island** — Michel Houellebecq (2005, France). Cloning, cults, and the end of humanity.
89. **Day of the Oprichnik** — Vladimir Sorokin (2006, Russia). A single day in a neo-medieval future Russia.
90. **Vita Nostra** — Marina & Sergey Dyachenko (2007, Ukraine). Dark, transformative "metaphysical" academy SF.
91. **The Core of the Sun** — Johanna Sinisalo (2013, Finland). A eugenic gender dystopia fueled by chili peppers.
92. **The Old Axolotl** — Jacek Dukaj (2015, Poland). Humanity survives only as digital uploads after an extinction event.
93. **The Employees** — Olga Ravn (2018, Denmark). A workplace SF told in eerie HR statements; Booker International–shortlisted.

## XI. Contemporary Global Voices (2005–2024)

94. **Never Let Me Go** — Kazuo Ishiguro (2005, UK/Japan). Quiet, heartbreaking SF about cloning and mortality.
95. **Station Eleven** — Emily St. John Mandel (2014, Canada). A traveling Shakespeare troupe after a pandemic.
96. **Severance** — Ling Ma (2018, China/US). Immigrant identity and office life amid an apocalypse.
97. **The Tiger Flu** — Larissa Lai (2018, China/Canada). Queer, post-plague feminist SF.
98. **Exhalation** — Ted Chiang (2019, China/US). Precise, profound speculative stories *(collection)*.
99. **How High We Go in the Dark** — Sequoia Nagamatsu (2022, Japan/US). Interlinked stories tracing a future plague.
100. **The Ministry of Time** — Kaliane Bradley (2024, UK/Cambodia). Time-travel, empire, and romance; a recent breakout.

### How this list spans the genre

- **Era:** From Cavendish (1666) and Shelley (1818) through 2024 releases.
- **Geography:** 25+ countries of origin, including China, Japan, Korea, India, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Thailand, Nigeria, Zambia, Egypt, Iraq, Palestine, Argentina, Cuba, Bolivia, Dominican Republic, Barbados, Poland, Russia, Ukraine, Finland, Denmark, France, Canada, and the US.
- **Subgenres:** Hard SF, space opera, cyberpunk, climate fiction, dystopia, first contact, time travel, biopunk, Afrofuturism, Indigenous futurisms, and literary/slipstream SF.
- **Authors:** A majority of women and nonbinary writers, extensive LGBTQ+ representation, and writers across many ethnic, national, and linguistic traditions.

*A reading list is always partial — this one trades some of the most familiar canonical names for a wider map of who has imagined the future, and where.*
